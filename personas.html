Key behavior pattern: The primary user actively avoids thinking about money in detail. They engage only when anxiety exceeds avoidance - a trigger event like a surprise charge or a renewal notification. The first session must convert avoidance into a moment of control. Post-persona research (June 2026) found clinical backing for this: people anticipate that engaging with their finances will increase anxiety, but studies show the opposite is true when done in a controlled, calm context. Source: Cornell University research, news.cornell.edu

Primary Persona

The Anxious Non-Looker

Primary
Emma, 29
The Anxious Non-Looker
Context

Emma works in marketing at a mid-size company in Berlin, London, or Chicago - this persona exists across markets. She earns a reasonable salary and considers herself reasonably responsible with money. She pays rent on time and has a small savings account.

She has subscriptions to Spotify, Netflix, an Adobe plan from a freelance project two years ago, a gym she visits irregularly, a meal-kit service she paused and forgot to cancel, a news site she clicked "annual plan" on during a sale, and two or three others she cannot immediately name. When she gets a notification about a charge, she mentally notes it and closes it without investigating.

Her relationship with money: "I probably spend more than I should on things I'm not using, but I don't want to know the exact number because it'll stress me out."

Jobs
When I suspect I'm paying for things I forgot about or never use, I want to see all my recurring charges clearly in one calm view, so that I feel in control of my money without needing to become a finance person. strategy.md Segment A JTBD, confirmed by C+R Research data (O2, O3) and user testimonials (O7)
When a subscription renews or a price changes, I want to know in plain language what happened, so I am never caught off guard by a charge on my statement. master-research.md J4, ux-patterns.md BP3 (action on surprise)
Pains
P1 - Vague background dread
She knows money is leaking out somewhere but the act of looking feels worse than the ignorance. She avoids her bank statement precisely because she fears what she'll find.
Updated in re-research (June 2026): Clinical research confirms two distinct triggers drive this avoidance. First, financial information acts as a "scarcity cue" that threatens her self-image as someone reasonably responsible with money - the information itself is the trigger, not the number. Second, fear of spiraling: "engaging will lead me into a complex, overwhelming situation." Both are design-addressable: judgment-free framing counters the first; one-thing-at-a-time progressive disclosure counters the second. Sources: PMC/NCBI 2024, The Financial Brand
P2 - Cryptic transaction names
When she does look, she sees "SPOTIFYAB STOCKHOLM" and "DCSF MEDIA GROUP" and cannot decode half the charges. This makes looking feel futile and stressful, not clarifying.
P3 - Bank connection as step 1
If a new app asks for her banking credentials in step 1, she closes it. She has tried twice and both times got to the "connect your bank" screen and thought: "I don't know these people yet." Four in five users were unaware apps store bank usernames and passwords. Source: bankingdive.com
P4 - No calm space to just see subscriptions
Every app she has tried feels overwhelming: too many numbers, too many graphs, too much emphasis on what she is doing wrong. The calm, focused subscription view does not exist. Source: competitive-analysis.md P2 (feature creep buries the core)
Trust Triggers
What earns trust
Showing value before asking for bank access - even a demo with sample data that looks like her life
Explicit plain-language explanation of what the product reads and why: "We read only recurring charge patterns. We cannot move money."
Real merchant names and logos in any preview - recognizing Spotify and Netflix immediately signals "this understands me"
Calm, non-urgent visual environment - nothing red, no words like "warning" or "overspending"
Testimonials from people who sound like her: "I found two subscriptions I forgot about and cancelled them"
What scares her off
Bank connection as step 1 - before any value is shown
Financial jargon or complexity on the first screen
Any suggestion she is doing something wrong with money
Graphs or percentage comparisons without plain-language context
Mood Quote
"The mental relief of knowing I'm not bleeding cash to forgotten subscriptions was priceless."
Rocket Money user. Source: productivewithchris.com
Secondary Persona

The Privacy-First Non-Looker

Secondary
Ravi, 31
The Privacy-First Non-Looker
Context

Ravi works in software or adjacent fields and is technically literate. He read an article about a data broker selling financial behavior data eighteen months ago and has been cautious about finance apps ever since. He uses a VPN and reads privacy policies when he cares enough.

He has the same subscription problem Emma has - more money going out than he realizes - but his barrier is not anxiety about the number. It is a principled refusal to route his bank credentials through any third party he does not personally trust. He tried Bobby (which he likes) but manual entry for 14+ subscriptions led him to abandon it twice after adding only half. He has not found an app that offers privacy AND automation. Source: ReSubs 18K+ users on no-bank model validates this segment, resubs.app

Jobs
When I want to understand my subscription spend, I want to track all my recurring charges without giving any app access to my bank account, so I feel in control of my money and secure at the same time. strategy.md Segment A2 JTBD, J6. Validated by ReSubs 18K+ users, resubs.app
When I need to cancel something, I want a step-by-step process that tells me exactly what to click, so I don't spend 30 minutes trying to find the cancel button. master-research.md J3, competitive-analysis.md (ReSubs 30+ cancel guides)
Pains
P1 - The manual entry trap
Adding 14+ subscriptions by hand is the single largest friction point. He has started twice and stopped when he ran out of patience. He knows the result would be worth it. He cannot get there without more automated help. Source: Bobby and ReSubs both show this friction; competitive-analysis.md P3
P2 - Privacy-or-nothing binary
The market offers full bank access (Rocket Money, Emma) or fully manual (Bobby). He wants automation without bank credentials. ReSubs' Gmail scan option introduces a different data concern. No product exists in the middle. Source: competitive-analysis.md P3
P3 - Incomplete picture as low-grade anxiety
Because manual entry is hard and he has not finished it, his current self-knowledge is "somewhere around $200/month on subscriptions, but probably more." This incompleteness creates its own background stress.
P4 - Hidden cancel complexity
Services he wants to cancel (gym, a streaming service) all have deliberately obscured cancellation flows. He has opened a tab to cancel and closed it frustrated. Source: competitive-analysis.md gap G4
Trust Triggers
What earns trust
Explicit "no bank connection required" messaging, visible before he has to click anything
Data-on-device storage with optional cloud backup. "Your data stays on your phone" is a strong signal. D2 closed
Manual entry with 400+ preset library at launch - reduces effort to a manageable level (founder decision D2, June 2026)
Transparent per-method data description: one sentence per import method explaining exactly what it accesses
No account required to try the product - let him start manually first
What scares him off
Any mention of Plaid - he knows what it accesses
Vague language like "bank-level security" without specifics
Required email registration before any value is shown
Data appearing to leave his device without explicit consent
Note on Gmail scan: It was hypothesized that Ravi would grant Gmail access more readily than bank access. Post-persona research found no direct evidence for this. Manual entry is confirmed as the most trusted method for privacy-first users. Gmail scan is deferred to v2, pending primary user testing. D2 closed Source: budgetvault.app
Mood Quote
"Haven't signed up yet. Not comfortable giving banking info to an app."
User expressing hesitation about bank-connected trackers. Source: 19pine.ai
Secondary Persona

The Motivated Cutter

Secondary
Claudia, 36
The Motivated Cutter
Context

Claudia is 36 and recently started counting her money more carefully. She is not financially anxious in the way Emma is - she is financially aware and slightly annoyed. She has two streaming services she barely uses, a project management tool from a brief freelance period, a cloud storage plan that upgraded itself, and a fitness app she replaced with a gym membership six months ago but somehow kept paying for.

She searched "subscription tracker app" on a Saturday morning after getting a renewal email for something she had forgotten about completely. She wants to find what she is paying for, decide what to cut, and be done in under 20 minutes. She is willing to connect her bank if it gets her to the result faster. Source: strategy.md Segment B, PocketGuard's 1M+ members validate this "what am I committed to" framing, pocketguard.com

Jobs
When I am looking to reduce my monthly spend, I want to quickly identify which subscriptions I actually use vs. which ones I keep forgetting to cancel, so I can free up money without a complicated process. strategy.md Segment B JTBD. 40.3% of subscribers cancelled at least one subscription in 2024, adapty.io
When I have decided to cancel something, I want a direct link or step-by-step guide so I can do it immediately while I am still motivated. master-research.md J3. Basic cancel instruction is free (founder decision D3, June 2026). Full guides are Pro.
Pains
P1 - Statement tedium
She knows the answer is in her bank statements somewhere. Scrolling through three months of transactions to find recurring charges is what made her look for an app instead. Consumers underestimate subscription spend by $133/month on average. Source: C+R Research 2022
P2 - Cancellation dark patterns
Every service she wants to cancel requires navigating to a buried settings page, clicking through a retention flow, and sometimes calling a phone number. She has started cancellations and given up. Source: competitive-analysis.md gap G4
P3 - Feature overload in existing tools
She tried Rocket Money briefly and felt it wanted her to do too much: budgets, credit scores, net worth. She just wants the subscription list. Source: competitive-analysis.md P2 (feature creep buries the core job)
Trust Triggers
What earns trust
Fast time-to-value: subscriptions visible in under 2 minutes of connecting her bank. Every extra step is drop-off risk.
Clear ROI framing: "You're paying $247/month before anything else" - the number and count together give her an instant decision signal.
Specific cancel help: a link and basic steps for the service she wants to cancel - free in all tiers. D3 closed
Concrete social proof with numbers: "Found 6 forgotten subscriptions totaling $437 in 15 minutes." CNET tester, via aol.com
What scares her off
Anything that adds steps to the cancellation process
Percentage-based fees for help ("we take 40% of what you save" - the Rocket Money model)
Having to wait for a human concierge when she wants to cancel now
Mood Quote
"After it saved me $400 in 15 minutes, I'd say it is."
Rocket Money user, responding to "Is Rocket Money worth it?" Source: finance.yahoo.com
