Jobs to Be Done

One main job. Five related jobs. Three emotional jobs. One social job. Two hypotheses ruled out. All critiqued and sourced.

The reason the product exists

Main Job

J-MAIN Fused J1 + J2 from strategy.md
"When I suspect I am paying for things I forgot about or never use, I want to see all my recurring charges clearly in one calm view, so that I feel in control of my money without needing to become a finance person."
42% of consumers forgot they were being charged for a subscription they no longer used. C+R Research 2022, huffpost.com
Consumers underestimate subscription spend by $133/month on average. C+R Research 2022, huffpost.com
"The mental relief of knowing I'm not bleeding cash to forgotten subscriptions was priceless." Rocket Money user, productivewithchris.com
No competitor has designed the first subscription view as a calm emotional experience. Every competitor leads with a dashboard, budget, or bank connection request. competitive-analysis.md P1, P2
Design updated June 2026 (D1): The reveal sequence is gradual, not a single total reveal. Count appears first, then categories with logos, then the total - shown paired with a concrete action on the same screen. The total is not a dramatic culmination. It is always accompanied by what the user can do about it. This converts the exposure from potential shock to calm agency. Clinical exposure therapy research supports this: gradual disclosure outperforms sudden full disclosure for anxiety reduction. Source: anxietycanada.com
Before, during, and after the main job

Related Jobs

J1
Activate without anxiety - the trust job
Personas: Emma (primary), Ravi (stronger version)
"When I land on a new app that asks for my financial data, I want to see what the product can do for me before I decide whether to trust it with my bank account, so that I only share my data when I have a clear reason to."
Evidence
Every bank-connected competitor asks for bank access in step 1 (competitive-analysis.md P1). 68% of users uncomfortable with apps' access to financial data. Banking Dive, bankingdive.com. 4 in 5 users did not know apps store bank usernames and passwords.
Product consequence
Two explicit activation paths (bank-connected and privacy-first) must both converge on the same aha moment. The trust job must be resolved in onboarding before the main job becomes accessible. Apple Card partially closes this job (benchmark C8 = 4/5 - shows value before commitment). No subscription tracker closes it fully.
J2
Identify and cancel unused subscriptions - the cut job
Personas: Claudia (primary), Emma and Ravi (secondary)
"When I am looking to reduce my monthly spend, I want to quickly identify which subscriptions I actually use vs. which ones I keep forgetting to cancel, so I can free up money without a complicated process."
Evidence
40.3% of subscribers cancelled at least one subscription in 2024. adapty.io. 48% forgot to cancel a free trial. The cancel-and-save moment is the most emotionally powerful moment in the product. "After it saved me $400 in 15 minutes, I'd say it is." Rocket Money user, finance.yahoo.com
Product consequence
Basic cancel instruction (link + step description) is free in all tiers - the relief moment must not be gated. Full guides, direct cancel links, and cancel confirmation tracking are Pro. Upgrade trigger lives here. D3 closed
Competitors
Rocket Money (concierge, but human-mediated and percentage-fee). ReSubs (30+ step-by-step cancel guides, no bank required). Hiatus. Neither offers the designed emotional moment of "you found money and it was easy to keep it."
J3
Understand what a charge actually is - the clarity job
Personas: Emma (primary - P2 pain), Claudia (secondary)
"When I see an unfamiliar charge on my statement, I want to know immediately what it is, which subscription it belongs to, and whether I authorized it, so that I can decide to keep it or act on it without having to investigate."
Evidence
Apple Card mechanism M3 (master-research.md) - real merchant names and logos replace cryptic bank codes. N26's EU merchant cleanup validates this as table stakes. support.apple.com, n26.com
Product consequence
Transaction enrichment - real names, logos, categories - is required at the subscription card level. Emma cannot feel relief if half the list says "SPOTIFYAB STOCKHOLM" or "DCSF MEDIA GROUP." This is activation-level quality, not a nice-to-have. Apple Card is best in class. Rocket Money uses Plaid enrichment. No subscription tracker has made this a front-page differentiator.
J4
Stay ahead of financial surprises - the alert job
Personas: Emma (primary), Claudia (secondary)
"When a subscription price changes, a payment fails, or a trial is about to convert, I want to be notified immediately in plain language, so I am never caught off guard by an unexpected charge."
Evidence
ux-patterns.md BP3: users act when notified, not when reminded to review. Monzo principle M1 (master-research.md): active voice in financial notifications ("we couldn't process your payment" not "payment declined"). Surprise charges trigger Emma's avoidance loop; being warned first converts the experience from threat to control.
Product consequence
Price-change and payment-failure alerts are retention architecture, not features. The weekly digest and push notifications are the return engine (ux-patterns.md BP4). No competitor has made notification design itself a trust-building experience. ? hypothesis: Monzo-style active-voice alerts build trust more than standard push
J5
Track subscriptions without sharing bank data - the privacy job
Personas: Ravi (primary), Emma (secondary - before trust is established)
"When I want to understand my subscription spend, I want to track all my recurring charges without giving any app access to my bank account, so I feel in control of my money and secure at the same time."
Evidence
ReSubs built 18,000+ users and 4.5 stars on Google Play entirely on a no-bank-connection model. resubs.app. 68% of users uncomfortable with app access to bank data. The privacy-or-convenience binary is a confirmed market gap (competitive-analysis.md P3).
Product consequence
Launch with manual entry + 400+ preset library. Gmail scan and AI screenshot import are deferred to v2, after user testing confirms they are trusted. The privacy path is a primary path, equal in visual weight to bank connection - not a fallback. D2 closed
Competitors
ReSubs (best in class, no bank required). Bobby (manual, iOS only). No competitor offers privacy AND automation in one product.
Under and alongside the functional jobs

Emotional & Social Jobs

E1
Feel financially competent without being a finance person
Persona: Emma (primary)
"When I use a money app, I want to feel like a capable adult who is on top of their finances, not like a person being judged for their spending choices, so that I keep using it and don't feel worse about myself."
Evidence
Avoidance behavior is partly about shame - looking at the real number feels like evidence of incompetence (personas.md P1, ux-patterns.md BP1). PMC/NCBI research confirms financial information triggers "threat to positive self-image." pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov. "The app helped me get organized and being prepared for monthly expenses helped alleviate some anxiety." Bobby user, justuseapp.com
Product consequence
Every screen must be judgment-free. No red. No language implying failure. The subscription list is presented as discovery, not audit. "What you have signed up for" is less threatening than "what you spent." Framing is a copywriting and design constraint, not a marketing choice.
E2
Experience the small win - the pride moment
Personas: Emma (after activation), Claudia (at cancellation)
"When I find a forgotten subscription or save money by cancelling, I want to feel the satisfaction of that moment, so that I associate the product with positive emotions and tell people about it."
Evidence
"Getting rid of those felt like a little pay raise." checkthat.ai. "I was shocked when I entered all my subscriptions and saw the yearly total." 19pine.ai user, 19pine.ai. The cancel-and-save moment is identified as the most shareable moment in the product (master-research.md key conclusion 3).
Product consequence
The cancel-win moment must be designed explicitly - not a generic success state but a moment with specific numbers: "You just freed up $14.99/month - that's $179.88/year." This is also where the share prompt lives. No competitor has designed this moment. The gap is confirmed.
E3
Feel safe sharing data - the trust maintenance job
Personas: Ravi (primary - ongoing), Emma (secondary)
"When I am using an app that holds my financial patterns, I want to know at any moment what it knows about me and be able to remove it completely, so that I never feel trapped or surveilled."
Evidence
Over half of consumers prefer built-in app settings to manage data permissions. Banking Dive, bankingdive.com. 4 in 5 users did not know apps store their bank credentials. Monarch Money makes "we do not sell your data" a front-page trust signal - no subscription tracker does this prominently.
Product consequence
Data deletion must be one tap. Data use must be explained in plain language on the first screen. These are not legal checkbox features - they are the trust infrastructure for both Emma and Ravi.
S1
Share the discovery - the social job ?
Personas: Emma (after activation), Claudia (after a cancel win)
"When I discover how much I'm spending on subscriptions, I want to share the number or the list with a friend, so that I feel connected to the experience and others can benefit from it too."
Evidence
Rocket Money's top testimonials all reference telling friends. Consistent with Spotify Wrapped-style share hypothesis (master-research.md H2). The specific format - subscription count + total spend, no bank data visible - is the privacy-safe shareable card.
Status
This is a hypothesis about virality. Real share rates are unknown. The product must design for shareability but cannot count on it. ? hypothesis - no direct share-rate evidence found
Jobs without sufficient evidence - ruled out

Hypotheses

JH1
Tracking shared household subscriptions ?
Out of scope
"When my partner and I want to understand our combined subscription spend, I want a shared view, so we stop paying for duplicates."
Why out of scope: Validated as a real need - Emma and Monarch both added shared modes. But confirmed as too complex for MVP. Every implementation (Monarch household mode, Emma collaborative budgeting 2025) requires heavy product complexity. More importantly, no interview data confirms that anxious millennials feel this job acutely before they have solved their own individual subscription visibility. The product must serve J-MAIN for one person before adding a second. Sources: monarch.com, emma-app.com
JH2
AI conversation about subscriptions ?
Not a fit for Segment A
"When I want to understand my spending patterns, I want to ask questions in plain language, so I can get answers without learning the app's interface."
Why ruled out: Monarch Money added an AI assistant. Copilot Money uses AI categorization. But ux-patterns.md explicitly rules this pattern out for our segment: conversational AI creates uncertainty for the anxious user rather than reducing it. The open-ended nature of a chat interface adds cognitive load - the opposite of Tendd's differentiator. This is a feature-push from the competitive landscape, not a pull from our users. No user evidence supports this desire in Segment A.

What the matrix tells us

3 MVP Core Jobs

Jobs that are (a) important for Emma, the primary persona, AND (b) not closed by the market.

MVP Core Job 1
J-MAIN + J1: See all recurring charges calmly + trust before bank
No competitor combines calm design, automatic detection, AND trust-building before bank connection. This is the clearest gap. The Guided Reveal onboarding with gradual disclosure and paired action is the product-level response.
MVP Core Job 2
J3: Understand what a charge actually is
Real merchant names and logos are table stakes that no subscription tracker has made a brand differentiator. Emma cannot feel relief if half the list says "AMZN MKTP US*AB12CD." This is activation-level quality - it must work on day one.
MVP Core Job 3
J5: Privacy path to the same aha moment
ReSubs confirms this segment exists and is willing to pay. No product offers privacy AND automation together. Building the privacy-first path with equal visual weight to bank connection closes a gap every bank-connected competitor ignores.

Candidate-for-Cut Functions

These close no MVP core job and should be cut.

Cut
AI conversation interface (JH2) - Closes no confirmed job for our personas. Adds cognitive load, the opposite of our differentiator.
Cut
Budget envelopes and spending goals - Addresses Segment B and C needs but not Segment A. Introduces complexity and judgment - the opposite of calm visibility.
Cut
Credit score monitoring - No job in our personas requires it. Signals "this is a full finance app" - actively hurts trust with Emma.
Cut
Investment tracking - Out of scope (CLAUDE.md). No persona job maps to it.
Cut from business model
Bill negotiation on percentage fee - Closes J2 but misaligns incentives. Profits from users having expensive problems; flat SaaS aligns our revenue with the user feeling calm and staying.

Danger List - All Closed

CLOSED - Founder Decision D1, June 14 2026
Danger 1: The reveal produces anxiety spike, not relief
Decision: gradual reveal with total paired with action on the same screen. Count first, then categories with logos, then total + next step. The total is never a standalone dramatic reveal. It is always accompanied by what the user can do about it. Converts exposure from shock to agency. Prototype test must use this sequence.
CLOSED AT DESIGN LEVEL - Validate in prototype
Danger 2: Financial framing triggers avoidance even in the product
Research confirmed avoidance is driven by (a) self-image threat from financial information and (b) fear of complexity spiraling. Both are addressed by: judgment-free language ("what you have signed up for" not "what you spent"), one-thing-at-a-time progressive disclosure, and total always paired with a concrete action. This is a design and copywriting decision. Prototype test confirms whether the handling is effective.
CLOSED - Founder Decision D2, June 14 2026
Danger 3: Gmail scan trust assumption is wrong
Decision: launch with manual + 400+ presets. Gmail scan is v2, after user testing. The question of whether Gmail scan is trusted more than bank access remains unanswered by web research and is moved to primary user research for the v2 decision. Not a wireframe or MVP blocker.

Three Questions for User Research

Q1 - Closes Danger 1
Does a gradual Guided Reveal produce relief for financially avoidant users?
Answer method: A/B prototype test with 5-8 people screened for financial-avoidance behavior. Version A: total first, then subscription list. Version B: count, then categories with logos, then total paired with action. Measure: completion rate, emotional state after, desire to see their own data. Pass signal: majority report relief and ask for the real thing.
Q2 - Closes Danger 2 (partially resolved, confirm in prototype)
Does framing subscriptions as "what you have signed up for" reduce anxiety more than "what you spent"?
Answer method: In the same prototype sessions as Q1, vary the headline copy and observe reactions. This is a low-cost test layered on top of Q1. Also test whether "committed" (PocketGuard model) feels controlling or clarifying to Segment A users.
Q3 - Closes Danger 3
For privacy-conscious users, does Gmail scan feel more or less invasive than bank connection?
Answer method: 5 users who self-identify as privacy-conscious. Present three import options with plain-language descriptions (no marketing language). Ask them to react. Capture specific fears. Determine whether Gmail scan is a path worth building at launch or better shipped as v2 after manual-first is validated.
